Who this is for: Independent craft sellers who feel their prices are "too high" to compete, but worry they're not covering costs. This is the framework I wish I'd had in year one. It works for yarn, ceramics, textiles, and most handmade goods.

I spent the first two years of my business chronically undercharging. I'd look at mass-produced alternatives, halve the difference, and call it a day. The result? I was working 50-hour weeks to earn less than minimum wage, and I couldn't understand why.

The turning point came when a mentor asked me: "Have you ever actually calculated your true hourly cost?" I hadn't. Once I did, everything changed. Here's the full system I now use to price every skein I dye.

The 5-Step Pricing Framework

1
Calculate your true material cost
Don't just count the yarn. Include dyes, mordants, water treatment, packaging (tissue, bags, tags, stickers), postage materials, and a proportional share of utilities for dyeing sessions. I track this per 100g skein using a simple spreadsheet.
💡 Include a 10% buffer for waste, mistakes, and material price fluctuations. What costs £X today may cost £X+12% in 6 months.
2
Set your target hourly wage — then honour it
Pick a real living wage for your area, not what you think "the market will bear." In the UK, the Real Living Wage (2026) is £13.85/hr outside London. Time everything: dyeing, rinsing, drying, skeining, photographing, listing, packing, admin. Every minute counts.
⏱ When I timed myself properly for the first time, I was shocked — a single skein took 47 minutes of active work, not the 20 I'd assumed.
3
Add your overhead allocation
Overheads are the costs you pay regardless of how much you sell: studio rent (or a proportion of home costs), insurance, website fees, marketplace fees, equipment depreciation, subscriptions. Divide your monthly overhead total by your monthly unit output. This is your overhead-per-unit.
4
Apply your profit margin
Profit is not the same as your wage. Profit is what allows you to invest back into the business — new equipment, stock, marketing, courses. I add a minimum 20% profit margin on top of all costs. This isn't greed; it's sustainability.
🌱 Without profit margin, you cannot grow. You can only maintain — and any unexpected cost will put you under.
5
Apply platform & transaction fees last
Most sellers forget this step. Etsy takes 6.5% + listing + payment processing. Adding fees at the end means you're absorbing them from your profit. Instead, gross up your price so that after fees, you still hit your target. The formula: Price ÷ (1 − fee rate).
💰 The Full Pricing Formula
Material Cost + Labour Cost + Overhead/Unit
= Base Cost
Base Cost × 1.20 = Cost + Profit
Cost + Profit ÷ (1 − 0.115) = Final Listing Price
*0.115 represents combined Etsy fees (~11.5%). Adjust for your platform. For selling direct (own website), use 0.03–0.05 for payment processing only.

A Real Example

Cost ElementAmount (per 100g skein)
Yarn base (100g merino)£5.80
Dyes, mordants, chemicals£1.20
Packaging (tissue, bag, label)£0.65
Utilities proportion£0.45
Labour (47 mins × £13.85/hr)£10.85

"But that's more than £28 for 100g — nobody will pay that!"
They will. The right customers will. And those are the only customers who will sustain your business long term. — The mindset shift that changed everything for me

When I raised my prices using this formula, I lost about 15% of my customers in the first month. But within three months my revenue had increased by 40%, because I was making the same or more with less volume, less stress, and customers who genuinely valued the work.
